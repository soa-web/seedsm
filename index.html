Why this works

The Core Idea

The LinkedIn Speak Translator went viral because of one thing: the painful recognition of language you live inside every day but never question. For HR leaders, there's an even richer vein — they are the human buffer between management speak and employee reality. They translate constantly, silently, exhaustingly. This series gives that experience a name, a laugh, and a home.

🎯
Recognition = Share
The moment an HR leader reads a translation and thinks "this is literally my Monday," they screenshot it. They tag a colleague. Shared pain is a social currency in HR circles.
😂
Funny = Reach
Humour bypasses the algorithm resistance to brand content. A translation post doesn't feel like an ad — it feels like a friend who works in HR finally said the quiet part out loud.
🪝
Series = Loyalty
Monday is Boss Translator day. A consistent cadence trains the audience to expect and look for SEED content. It builds a community ritual, not just impressions.
🌱
CTA = Soft Sell
HR leaders hate being sold to. Earn trust with content they love, then whisper the CTA at the end. The ratio should feel like: 90% relatable, 10% SEED. That's when they click.
The Big Questions

What We Need to Figure Out with Timmy & Sam

Before locking the calendar, these are the insight questions worth testing with your HR leader contacts — they'll sharpen which translations land hardest.

Q1: What's the corporate phrase you translate in your head every day?
This is the gold mine question. Ask Timmy and Sam to brainstorm 5–10 phrases their CEO, CFO, or line managers use regularly — and what those phrases actually mean in practice. The translations that will go viral are the ones that are hyper-specific (not generic jargon) and have a gap between the official meaning and the real meaning. E.g. "We're family here" → "Expect unpaid overtime and emotional labour." The specificity is the humour.
Q2: What kind of content would you repost on your own LinkedIn right now?
HR leaders curate their own brand carefully. Ask: would they share a meme? A text-only translation post? A video? A poll? Understanding their sharing comfort level tells you the format priority. Most likely: short-form text with a surprising translation headline does well because it's professional enough for LinkedIn but punchy enough to share. Video may work better for Instagram/TikTok repost days (Tuesday).
Q3: Is there a specific situation this week that drove you crazy — that you wish someone had trained leadership on?
This reverse-engineers the CTA. If Timmy says "my CEO announced restructuring in an all-hands with zero preparation for how HR should respond" — that is a content idea AND a CTA hook. The translation post becomes "CEO says 'exciting restructuring ahead' → HR hears [panic] [no playbook] [employees in my DMs at 10pm]" → CTA: SEED Academy's Leadership Communication Training.
Q4: What do you wish your C-suite actually understood about your job?
This surfaces the deep frustration that HR leaders rarely get to voice publicly. Turn those frustrations into the most emotionally resonant translation posts. These are the ones that get 200+ comments because other HR leaders pile on with "THIS." They also position SEED as a training partner that actually sees HR — not just a vendor selling courses.

Where to Take This

Expansion Directions

The "HR Translation" concept is a platform, not just a post series. Here are 6 ways to scale it once the weekly cadence is established — from digital tools to events.

01
Build an Actual HR Translator Tool
Inspired directly by the LinkedIn Speak Translator — build a simple web tool where HR leaders paste in a corporate email/memo and get the "HR Translation" back. Viral by design. Free to use. SEED-branded. Can be embedded in LinkedIn posts ("try our free translator"). Drives direct traffic to SEED's site and captures leads through a subscribe gate for the full translation report.
Example hook: "Paste in your CEO's last all-hands summary. We'll tell you what HR actually needs to do about it."
02
The HR Translation Glossary (Lead Magnet)
A downloadable PDF or microsite: "50 Corporate Phrases, Translated for HR." Free download in exchange for email. Circulates on LinkedIn as a shareable document. Every entry ends with a training nudge. Acts as a soft pitch deck disguised as a comedy document. Can be repurposed as a talk intro at HR events or a leave-behind at conferences.
Example entry: "We're investing in our people" → Translation: "We are about to ask HR to design a recognition programme with a budget of zero and launch it in two weeks."
03
"Live Translation" on LinkedIn Events
Host a monthly 30-minute LinkedIn Live where SEED invites 1 HR leader guest (Timmy, Sam, etc.) to do live translation of 5 real (anonymised) corporate memos, Slack messages, or job postings. Funny, fast, interactive with live audience comments. Ends with a SEED pitch in the last 3 minutes. Creates 5 clips for the next month of Tuesday Trend Translation posts.
Format: "Corporate said: [reads memo]. Our guest HR translator: [reads translation]. Audience votes: too real / too kind / you missed the worst part."
04
Employee Perspective Edition
Flip the direction: translate what employees say to HR — and what they actually mean. "I'm looking for growth opportunities" = "My manager hasn't spoken to me in 4 months." This creates a second content track that's just as shareable, positions SEED as understanding the full employee experience, and opens up a separate audience (employees who want to share with their HR teams).
Series name suggestion: "The Employee-to-HR Dictionary" — pairs with the Boss Translator as a weekly alternating guest edition.
05
Industry-Specific Translation Packs
Once the general series has traction, create industry editions: "Banking Translation," "Tech Startup Translation," "Manufacturing HR Translation." The specific language shifts per industry (banks say "governance," startups say "velocity," manufacturers say "operational efficiency") and so do the translations. These can be pitched as sponsored editions to industry associations, expanding SEED's reach beyond organic LinkedIn.
Tech startup example: "We move fast and break things" → Translation: "We don't have an HR policy, an employment contract template, or a clue, and I've just been promoted to run it."
06
The Annual "Untranslatable" Award
Run a yearly campaign: "Submit the most untranslatable corporate phrase of the year." Public voting, winner gets featured in a proper designed post + a free SEED training seat for their team. Generates massive UGC, press-worthy, and positions SEED as a community institution — not just a vendor. Ideal to run in December when HR content engagement spikes.
Campaign name: "The SEED Decoder Awards" — "Because some things in HR need more than a translator."
